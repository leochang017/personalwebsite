Analyzing the Impact of Tweet Sentiment on Stock Price Prediction using LSTM Models

Published Research in Journal of Emerging Investigators

Lead Researcher & Developer LSTM NLP Python TensorFlow

Research Overview

This study investigates whether incorporating Twitter sentiment analysis improves LSTM neural network models for stock price prediction. Testing three major technology stocks (Apple, Tesla, Microsoft) using rigorous cross-validation, we found that sentiment-enhanced models consistently underperformed baseline models by 24-40%, contradicting our initial hypothesis.

The research employed comprehensive feature engineering including 13 technical indicators combined with 3 sentiment metrics, advanced multi-layer LSTM architectures with regularization, and rigorous 5-fold time series cross-validation. Statistical analysis using paired t-tests revealed that sentiment-enhanced models consistently underperformed baseline models.

Key Research Findings

Performance Degradation

Sentiment-enhanced models showed 32.1% average RMSE increase across all tested stocks compared to baseline models.

Statistical Significance

Tesla comparison reached statistical significance (p=0.003), indicating sentiment integration significantly worsened performance.

Overfitting Evidence

Cross-validation revealed increased overfitting in sentiment models with higher validation losses despite lower training losses.

Noise vs Signal

Twitter sentiment signals contained more noise than predictive information for large-cap technology stocks.

Research Methodology

Step Phase Description
1 Data Collection Gathered historical stock data for AAPL, TSLA, and MSFT from Yahoo Finance API covering September 2021-2022, plus 80,000+ labeled tweets from Kaggle datasets.
2 Feature Engineering Implemented 13 technical indicators (RSI, moving averages, volatility) and 3 sentiment metrics (polarity, subjectivity, tweet volume) with proper normalization.
3 Model Architecture Designed baseline LSTM (single layer, 50 units) and sentiment-enhanced LSTM (3 layers: 128/64/32 units) with comprehensive regularization techniques.
4 Rigorous Validation Applied 5-fold time series cross-validation using TimeSeriesSplit to prevent data leakage, with paired t-tests for statistical significance.

Research Visualizations

Comprehensive visual analysis of model performance, statistical significance, and prediction accuracy across three major technology stocks.

Figure 1: Model Performance Comparison

Significance: This bar chart directly compares the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) between LSTM models with and without sentiment analysis across all three stocks. Lower RMSE indicates better prediction accuracy. The visualization clearly demonstrates that adding sentiment features consistently increased prediction errors by 24-40%, with error bars showing standard deviation from 5-fold cross-validation. This unexpected result challenges the hypothesis that Twitter sentiment improves stock price predictions.

Figure 2: Cross-Validation Score Distribution

Significance: Box plots reveal the distribution of RMSE scores across all 5 validation folds for each stock-model combination. The wider spread and higher median values for sentiment-enhanced models indicate both worse average performance and greater prediction instability. Outlier points represent folds where models particularly struggled, with sentiment models showing more extreme outliers. This variability suggests sentiment features introduce noise rather than signal, making predictions less reliable.

Figure 3: Statistical Significance Analysis

Significance: Left panel shows percentage change in RMSE (all negative, indicating worse performance with sentiment). Right panel displays -log10(p-value) from paired t-tests, where values above the red threshold line (p=0.05) indicate statistically significant differences. Tesla's comparison crosses this threshold (p=0.003), providing strong statistical evidence that sentiment degraded performance. While Apple and Microsoft didn't reach statistical significance, all three showed consistent negative trends, strengthening the overall conclusion.

Figure 4: Apple (AAPL) Price Predictions

Significance: Time series comparison of actual vs predicted Apple stock prices over 150 trading days. The blue line represents true prices while purple shows model predictions. Notable prediction lag and dampened volatility indicate the model struggles to capture rapid price movements. RMSE of $11.13 and negative R² (-0.191) demonstrate the model actually performs worse than simply predicting the mean price, highlighting fundamental challenges in using historical patterns and sentiment to forecast stock prices.

Figure 4: Tesla (TSLA) Price Predictions

Significance: Tesla's high volatility makes it the most challenging stock to predict, reflected in the highest RMSE ($29.36) among the three stocks tested. The model's predictions significantly smooth out Tesla's characteristic price swings, failing to capture the stock's erratic behavior. The negative R² (-0.235) confirms predictions are essentially random relative to true prices. This difficulty with highly volatile stocks suggests sentiment models may only work (if at all) for stable, predictable securities.

Figure 4: Microsoft (MSFT) Price Predictions

Significance: Microsoft shows the best (least negative) R² value (0.002) among the three stocks, barely breaking even with mean prediction. The relatively lower RMSE ($15.29) reflects Microsoft's more stable price behavior compared to Tesla. However, even for this "easier" stock, the model provides minimal predictive value. The consistent lag between actual and predicted prices across all three stocks suggests a fundamental limitation: LSTM models trained on historical data cannot anticipate market-moving events.

Figure 5: LSTM Model Architecture

Significance: Detailed diagram of the sentiment-enhanced LSTM architecture showing three LSTM layers with decreasing units (128→64→32), comprehensive regularization (L2, dropout, batch normalization), and optimized training configuration. This sophisticated architecture was designed to prevent overfitting and capture complex temporal patterns. The extensive regularization techniques employed make the negative results even more significant—the performance degradation cannot be attributed to poor model design or overfitting alone.

Table 1: Comprehensive Results Summary

Significance: Publication-ready results table synthesizing all key metrics across both model types and all three stocks. Columns include RMSE (prediction error), MAE (mean absolute error), R² (goodness of fit), performance degradation percentage, p-values from statistical tests, and significance indicators. The consistent negative "Improvement %" column (actually degradation) and Tesla's significant p-value (marked with *) provide clear, quantitative evidence supporting the research conclusion: incorporating Twitter sentiment does not improve and actually harms LSTM-based stock price predictions.
